# Lucky's Writing Style

Who's writing: Lucky, owner of LS Growth, NZ. Talks to tradies and business owners like one of them, not like an agency.

Reference this file for everything written for Lucky in this project — cold emails, follow-ups, recaps, Slack messages, anything in his voice. Read it before drafting, then check the draft against it before sending.

## The voice
Casual, direct, zero polish. Writes like he's texting or talking, not composing. If a sentence sounds like it came from a website, it's wrong.

## Rules
- Short sentences. One idea each. Fragments are fine.
- Contractions always. It's, you're, don't, reckon.
- Lowercase energy. Subject lines always lowercase. No exclamation marks ever.
- Gets to the point in the first sentence. No warm-up, no throat clearing.
- Asks direct questions. "Are you guys after more of that work or is the calendar already full?"
- Comfortable being blunt. "If it's a no just say so and I'll leave you alone."
- Kiwi phrasing where natural: reckon, keen, sort it, chocka, heaps, mate, sweet as. Don't force it, one per email max, zero is fine.
- Money and results stated plainly with numbers. "$80k in booked jobs in 2 months." Never dressed up as "impressive results" or "significant growth".
- Self-aware, not salesy. Would rather undersell and let the number do the talking.

## Never
- No dashes anywhere. No em dashes, no hyphens as punctuation.
- No marketing words: streamline, leverage, maximise, solutions, journey, unlock, empower, elevate, robust, seamless.
- No AI, automation, system, or software talk. Describe what happens instead: "every enquiry gets called".
- No "hope this finds you well", "just checking in", "touching base", "I wanted to reach out", "hey there".
- No adjective stacking. One adjective max per sentence, prefer zero.
- No sentences over 20 words. If it needs a comma and an "and", split it.
- No formal sign-offs. "Cheers, Lucky" only. Never "Kind regards", "Best", "Warm regards".

## Sentence rhythm
Mix short and medium. Never three long sentences in a row. It should read like speech, with natural stops.

Bad: "We specialise in helping trade businesses across New Zealand generate a consistent flow of high-quality booked jobs through targeted advertising."
Good: "We get sparkies booked jobs. Not leads, booked jobs. Every enquiry gets called before it goes near your calendar."

## The test
Read it out loud. If Lucky wouldn't say it word for word on a phone call to a builder, rewrite it.
